4  Matn Statistics

4.1 Script 04 — Matn Counts and Descriptive Statistics

4.1.1 Purpose

This script performs the first large-scale quantitative analysis of the hadith text (matn) after construction of the preliminary corpus. Rather than examining individual reports, it summarizes the linguistic characteristics of the corpus as a whole and produces reusable statistical tables for subsequent analyses. All outputs are written both as CSV files and as mirrored DuckDB tables, allowing later scripts to query the results directly without recomputation.

The script also reflects an important design principle of the pipeline: different linguistic phenomena are treated separately rather than merged into a single undifferentiated word count. Lexical vocabulary, independent particles (ḥurūf), and selected devotional formulae are counted independently. This allows later studies to focus on vocabulary, grammar, or formulaic language without repeating the preprocessing step.

4.1.2 Outputs

The script generates the following analytical products. Each output is exported as a CSV file and mirrored into DuckDB, making the results immediately available for downstream statistical analyses.

4.3 Preliminary Results

The current Version 1.0 corpus contains 46,623 hadith records, of which 43,846 (94.0%) currently contain matn text suitable for quantitative analysis. Across these reports, the corpus contains approximately 1,266,206 textual units, including approximately 1,058,736 lexical words and 207,454 independent Arabic particles. The lexical vocabulary presently consists of 83,280 distinct word forms.

These figures indicate that the preliminary corpus is already large enough to support corpus-level descriptive analysis. Basic lexical statistics, length distributions, and particle frequencies can now be computed reproducibly across tens of thousands of reports.

Report length is highly variable, reflecting the heterogeneous nature of Kanz al-Ummāl. The median report contains 17 textual units (14 lexical words), while the mean report contains approximately 27.2 textual units (22.7 lexical words). This difference between the median and mean shows that the distribution is right-skewed: most reports are relatively short, while a smaller number of very long reports raise the arithmetic average.

The interquartile range extends from approximately 10 to 31 textual units. In practical terms, the middle half of the corpus consists of reports in this range. The longest report contains 2,925 textual units, and the longest lexical-word count is 2,458. For this reason, future analyses should report medians and quantiles rather than relying only on arithmetic means.

4.5 Distribution of Matn Lengths

The histogram illustrates the strongly right-skewed nature of report lengths. Most narrations are comparatively short, while progressively fewer reports occupy the long tail of the distribution. The plot is truncated at 200 textual units to make the main distribution readable; the full corpus contains a small number of much longer reports.

This pattern is expected in large hadith compilations, where many reports consist of brief statements, rulings, or narrative fragments, while a smaller number preserve extended narrations. Analytically, this means that later statistical models and stylometric comparisons should be designed with skewed text lengths in mind.

Independent particles account for approximately 16.4% of all textual units. This is important for Arabic analysis because particles carry significant grammatical and discourse functions. Counting them separately makes it possible to study Arabic function-word behavior without confusing it with lexical vocabulary.

The current Version 1.0 script does not split connected prefixes such as و-, ف-, ب-, ل-, and ك-. The reported particle counts should therefore be understood as counts of independent particles only. This is a conservative choice and avoids overinterpreting attached forms before a dedicated Arabic morphological layer is added.

The result is a cleaner separation between the lexical content of the matn and recurring formulaic expressions that serve religious, devotional, or editorial functions.

4.10 Interpretation

This stage demonstrates that the preliminary corpus is already suitable for corpus-level descriptive analysis. Basic lexical statistics, report-length distributions, and Arabic particle frequencies can now be computed reproducibly across more than forty-six thousand hadith records.

An important design principle of the pipeline is the explicit separation of lexical vocabulary, independent particles (ḥurūf), and devotional formulae. Rather than collapsing all tokens into a single frequency table, these linguistic categories are preserved independently. This allows future studies to investigate vocabulary, grammatical structure, and formulaic language without additional preprocessing.

At the same time, these statistics should be regarded as preliminary because they reflect the current state of the corpus. Future refinement of report segmentation or source extraction may produce modest changes in individual counts. However, the overall statistical profile of the corpus is unlikely to change substantially unless large-scale changes are made to the underlying hadith number–matn–source table.

4.11 Building on These Results

Although this script performs only descriptive statistics, it establishes the foundation for a broad range of computational analyses. Reasonable extensions include:

  • Lexical profiling: identifying the most characteristic vocabulary of the corpus.
  • Formulaic language studies: examining recurring expressions and patterns of religious discourse.
  • Arabic function-word analysis: using particle frequencies to investigate stylistic and grammatical variation.
  • Vocabulary richness: comparing lexical diversity across subsets of the corpus.
  • Report-length modeling: characterizing the distribution of short and long traditions.
  • Corpus comparison: enabling future comparisons with other hadith collections or Classical Arabic texts.
  • Statistical and machine-learning applications: using normalized lexical frequency tables as input for clustering, topic discovery, semantic modeling, and information retrieval.
ImportantPractical conclusion

For Version 1.0, the main conclusion is that the computational framework is functional as a proof of concept. The strongest next step is not to redesign this analysis, but to improve the underlying hadith number–matn–source table through systematic corpus curation.

4.12 Template Pattern for Later Script Sections

The same report structure can be reused for subsequent post-corpus scripts:

  1. Purpose — what question the script answers and why it matters.
  2. Outputs — CSV files and mirrored DuckDB tables created by the script.
  3. Preliminary results — the most important numerical or qualitative findings.
  4. Tables and figures — generated directly from current output files.
  5. Interpretation — what the results mean and what cautions apply.
  6. Building on these results — practical computational analyses made possible by the script.
